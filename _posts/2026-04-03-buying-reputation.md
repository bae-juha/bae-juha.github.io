**by Lingfang Li, Steven Tadelis, and Xiaolan Zhous** *(The RAND Journal of Economics, 2020)*

---

> **Abstract**
> *Seller reputation, generated by buyer feedback, is critical to fostering trust in online marketplaces. Marketplaces or sellers may choose to compensate buyers for providing feedback. Signaling theory predicts that only sellers of high-quality products will reward buyers for truthful feedback, especially when a product lacks any feedback and when the seller is not established. We confirm these hypotheses using Taobao's reward-for-feedback mechanism. High-quality products, especially without established feedback, are chosen for feedback rewards, which cause sales to increase by 36%. Marketplaces and consumers can therefore benefit from allowing sellers to buy feedback and signal their high-quality products in the process.*

## **1. Summary**

This paper studies whether sellers can “buy” reputation in a way that credibly signals product quality. Using proprietary data from Taobao’s reward-for-feedback (RFF) mechanism, the authors argue that offering buyers a rebate for leaving **informative** feedback can work like a signal: high-quality sellers are more willing to pay for feedback because they expect positive and detailed reviews, while low-quality sellers have less incentive to do so. Consistent with this signaling logic, sellers are more likely to adopt RFF for high-quality items, especially for cold-start products with no established feedback, and less likely to rely on it when the seller already has a strong reputation.

The paper also finds that buyers respond strongly to this signal. Items with RFF have about **36% higher sales**, receive more detailed and informative reviews, and get feedback faster, while the mechanism does **not** appear to bias ratings toward being more positive. The authors therefore conclude that a platform-mediated reward-for-feedback system can both improve information provision and help solve the cold-start problem in online marketplaces, benefiting sellers, buyers, and the platform itself.

## **2. Comments**

This paper corresponds directly to my interest: online review, its informativeness and the bias. The most interesting two factors are that the 'RFF' system introduced in this paper determines how informative the feedback is, and how the system rebates buyers regardless of whether the feedback is positive or negative. It also shows the data that the adoption of RFF does not lead to the bias towards the positive feedbacks. <br>
I have been thinking about the 'review' system of Korean delivery platforms for months. The biggest concern about those platforms' review system is that, the sellers provide extra foods (cheeseballs, for example) and ask for **positive** reviews, which leads to the decline on the credibility for those reviews. In fact, most of the stores have 4.9~5.0 ratings out of 5, which clearly shows how meaningless it became. <br>
The buyers are not 'forced' to write (positive) reviews, though. Sellers cannot track whether a buyer wrote a review or not. It is completely up to the buyers whether to write a review, and moreover, whether to be positive or not. It is completely fine to write negative reviews even if you got some extra chips for the promise to write a positive one. Interesting phenomenon is that, despite this issue, people actually tend to 'write' the reviews, although they are not forced to. Weird, right? <br>
Nonetheless, this 'review event' system is now prevailing in most of the stores. Even I, when choosing which store to order my fried chicken, first see whether this store provides such rewards or not. One **positive effect** of this is that the market is actually becoming more competitive! Stores, as they cannot choose the price by itself, started to provide more and more free stuffs, which is almost equivalent to lowering the price. This is why I cannot claim that we should ban this review event system. <br>
This paper showed an example of how brilliant the platform 'Taobao' solved this problem. It made sure that the reviews are not biased to the positive side, and the data actually shows the unbiasedness. I still have doubts where this unbiasedness would work in Korea though. Once we are rewarded, it feels a little bit mean to write negative reviews, rather choosing not to write one at all. <br>
One more comment. I always think about the distribution of the ratings. The distribution is kinda funny, because most of the ratings are concentrated on either 1 or 10. If you liked it, you give it a full score. If it was just okay, you choose not to rate it. If it was so bad, you rate it the minimum, even it was not 'that' bad. My suggestion is to nudge the rating system by placing the default location of the rating point right at the middle, and letting the buyer to move the pointer. Wouldn't this make the distribution actually reflect the quality of the product?
